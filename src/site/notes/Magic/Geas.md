A **Geas** *(pronounced “Geash” or “Geish”,plur. Geasa)* is a sort of trade with the Weave. 

In its most basic form, a Geas is a vow where the user gives up something and gains something of at most the same worth, though the user doesn’t need to gain anything. Geasa without gain are often simply called Vows. To the [[Weave\|Weave]], something is worth as much as you value it, though it seems to, by principle, not accept mortal currencies, and emotional worth weighs much heavier than material worth.

Making a Geas usually requires a ritual of some kind. To make a Geas, one must touch the Weave(touching the Weave for prolonged amounts of time is surprisingly difficult) and negotiate the terms of the Geas with it. This is the most robust way of making a Geas, though the Weave will only let you know if what you’re offering is not enough, never if it is too much. There are never counteroffers.  
A simpler but much less robust way to make a Geas is to simply say the terms out loud or write them over and over while being in contact with the Weave, until you feel that the Geas has been placed. This way, there’s no way to know exactly how much the Weave will take, but it's usually faster. When it can, the Weave tends to abuse lazy Geasmaking, taking more than it needs or twisting the words to give less than it should. This is why the first method is more robust, as relying on words naturally leads to ambiguity.

Geasa can never be undone, and the [[Weave\|Weave]] never gives something back, even through another Geas. 
The consequence of breaking a Geas is usually [[Dissolution\|Dissolution]] into the Weave, though in many cases reality bends itself to make breaking the Geas impossible.

*Example: Geremy who vowed to never step inside again, upon trying to step inside a house is blocked by an invisible wall.*

*Example 2: Pete who vowed to always accept what he is handed, upon refusing a beer violently erupts into purple flames and is never seen again, to the horror of his drinking mates.*

There are broadly speaking three categories of Geasa: [[Magic/Geas#Personal Geasa\|Personal Geasa]], [[Magic/Geas#Pathway Geasa\|Pathway Geas]], and [[Magic/Geas#Exchange Geasa\|Exchange Geasa]].  
Personal Geasa are bound to a single person, while Pathway Geasa are bound to a Pathway and all of its members. 

#### Pathway Geasa

Pathway Geasa are by far the most common. Without Pathway Geasa, a [[Magic/Pathway\|Pathway]] is practically worthless, as its only worth is the potential it holds. 

Pathway Geasa must always target all of the members, and all of the members must be present and must make the Geas together.  
The forms of Pathway Geasa are Cisdimensional Geasa and Transdimensional Geasa. Cisdimensional Geasa affect only the Weave part of the Pathway and doesn’t impact any of its members directly. Usually it gains the user power by giving up potential other power.

*Example: Bob the Mayomancer gives up the power over nature to gain stronger mayo magic.*
  
Transdimensional Geasa affect both the Pathway itself and its members. 

Example: John the Sauceror gives up his family heirloom sauce recipe, fully forgetting the recipe and never being able to learn it again, to gain the power to conjure sauces.

The consequence of breaking a minor Pathway Geas is often milder than breaking a regular Geas. It usually only results in the loss of your Pathway, though that can also make you a [[Weavetangler\|Weavetangler]], making magic around you act unpredictably, or even blocking it completely.

Finally, when you make a Pathway, it is not uncommon to, because you already have ideas about your Pathway, accidentally make minor Pathway Geasa, called Innate Geasa.

  

#### Personal Geasa

Personal Geasa are bound to one person. They do not affect a [[Magic/Pathway\|Pathway]].  
These can be pretty much anything, but they must still follow the rule that what is gained cannot be of greater worth than what is lost.

*Example: John Lick the Assassin vows to never pick up a blade longer than his forearm to become invisible in the shadows.*

###### Imposed Geasa

Imposed Geasa are a subtype of [[Magic/Geas#Personal Geasa\|Personal Geasa]], wherein a third party makes the Geas and the first party only accepts it. For this, the only conditions are that the first party is conscious while hearing, reading or otherwise perceiving the Geas in a language they know and the first party voluntarily accepting it. For written Imposed Geasa, they need to be written while in contact with the Weave.

*Example: Marcus of Mount Sucrose wants you to accept his lengthy written Geas allowing him access to your senses at any time, before he allows you to use his messenger stone network.*

*Example 2: In courts in the [[Empire\|Empire]], it is custom for witnesses to accept a Geas that they will not lie for the duration of the court session and in turn be invulnerable to nonmagic weapons. This Geas is written on a sign on the witness entrance of the courtroom with a disclaimer that by entering through there as a witness you agree to the Geas.*

The [[Weave\|Weave]] is very lenient when it comes to the definition of “voluntarily”. Often, it even works under torture. As long as the first party has a choice in the matter, even if the alternative to accepting is death or worse, the Weave considers it “voluntarily”.

#### Exchange Geasa

Exchange Geasa are Geasa where the loss of one party is the gain of the other and vice versa. Both parties need to agree, similarly to Imposed Geasa, or the Geas needs to be made by both parties in conjunction.

*Example: A blind person gives a deaf person half of his hearing for half of the deaf person’s vision.*

#### Quirks

Due to the fact that something is worth what you value it, someone who would give everything and more to be a powerful mage will never be so, while someone who wouldn’t dream of becoming a mage will easily become a powerful mage.  
Many universities actively discourage students from pursuing the path they choose.
Most powerful mages are not the mages they would wish to be. Some die trying to undo their Geasa, some take the easy way out and commit suicide(which is frighteningly easy to do when all it takes is a spell gotten out of hand)
